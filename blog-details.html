How to Maintain Constant Fermentation Temperatures

By Thomas Kray • Published on June 18, 2026

Yeast is a living organism. When brewing beer, yeast metabolizes sugars to produce alcohol and carbon dioxide. However, during this process, it also creates aromatic chemical compounds called esters and phenols. While some esters are desirable (like the banana aroma in a Hefeweizen), uncontrolled fluctuations in temperature can stress the yeast, causing it to produce harsh fusel alcohols and solvent-like off-flavors.

1. Why Room Temperature Isn't Enough

Many beginner brewers believe that if their room thermometer reads 68°F (20°C), their fermentation is safe. However, yeast fermentation is an **exothermic reaction**—meaning it releases heat. At the peak of active fermentation, the internal temperature of a 5-Gallon batch can rise up to 5 to 8 degrees higher than the surrounding ambient air temperature.

2. The Solutions: From Budgets to Pros

Depending on your budget and space limits, there are three key ways to control your heat profile:

  • The Swamp Cooler (Low Cost): Placing your fermenter in a large tub of water and wrapping it in wet towels with ice packs. Requires constant manual monitoring.
  • Modified Refrigerators (Medium Cost): Plugging a standard chest freezer or refrigerator into a digital temperature controller (like an Inkbird) with a heating wrap wrapped around the fermenter.
  • Jacketed Conicals & Glycol (Professional): Pumping chilled food-grade glycol through stainless steel jacket lines wrapped around a conical fermenter. This is the gold standard for microbreweries.
"Maintaining temperature constancy during the first 72 hours of active fermentation handles over 90% of yeast-derived off-flavor concerns." - Thomas Kray
